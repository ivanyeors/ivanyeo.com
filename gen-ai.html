How Generative AI Works: Text to Image

My first challenge was understanding the fundamental process of how text prompts are transformed into images. This process is more complex than I initially imagined:

×

The text-to-image generation process involves several sophisticated steps. First, a text encoder (typically CLIP) converts your text prompt into a numerical representation that captures its semantic meaning. Then, a diffusion model gradually transforms random noise into a coherent image guided by that text embedding.

What fascinated me was how these models understand relationships between concepts. For example, when you prompt "a cat wearing a space helmet," the model needs to understand what cats and space helmets look like, and how they would realistically combine. This represents a remarkable level of conceptual understanding.

Text Encoding

The prompt is encoded into a numerical representation using models like CLIP that understand language concepts

Diffusion Process

Starting from random noise, the model gradually refines the image through multiple denoising steps

Sampling Methods

Different sampling algorithms (Euler, DPM, etc.) control how the noise is removed, affecting image quality and style

Common Terminologies in Generative AI

While picking up image generative AI software, there are some terms that often show up while using them. Having a better understanding of these terms will help facilitate more control over the images we want to generate:

×

Checkpoint (ckpt)

A saved state of a trained model. Different checkpoints specialize in different styles or subject matter. For example, some are better at realistic portraits, while others excel at anime styles.

CLIP

Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training. This is the part of the model that understands your text prompt and translates it into a form the image generator can use to guide the creation process.

VAE

Variational Auto-Encoder. This component compresses images into a compact representation (latent space) and then decodes them back. It's responsible for the final image quality and color reproduction.

Latent Space

A compressed mathematical representation where images exist as points or vectors. The diffusion process occurs in this space, which is much more efficient than working with full-resolution pixel data.

Understanding Sampling Methods

I discovered that sampling methods significantly impact image generation. These algorithms control how the model transforms noise into an image:

Euler

A fast sampling method that produces good results with fewer steps. Great for quick iterations and testing prompts.

DPM Solver

A more advanced sampling method that can achieve higher quality results with fewer steps compared to traditional methods.

Sampling Steps

The number of iterations the diffusion process goes through. More steps generally mean higher quality but take longer to generate.

CFG Scale

Classifier-Free Guidance scale. This controls how closely the image follows your prompt. Higher values create images that match your prompt more closely but may look less natural.

Learning these terms transformed my ability to control and customize the generation process. What once seemed like random technical jargon now represented powerful tools for image refinement.

How ControlNet Works

One of the most exciting breakthroughs in my research was discovering ControlNet, which adds remarkable control over the generation process:

×

ControlNet allows for precise control over image generation by using conditioning inputs like edges, depth maps, or pose estimation. This technology essentially lets you "guide" the diffusion model by providing structural information that the generated image should follow.

×

I spent hours experimenting with different ControlNet modules and discovered how they could dramatically improve the reliability and precision of image generation:

Canny Edge

Uses edge detection to maintain the structural outlines of an image or sketch

Depth Map

Controls the spatial relationships and perspective in the generated image

Pose Control

Guides the positioning of human figures based on skeletal keypoints

Segmentation

Defines regions of the image for specific content based on color-coded masks

×

The power of ControlNet becomes evident when trying to maintain compositional consistency across multiple generations. For designers, this is invaluable when creating variations of a concept while keeping core elements in place.

×

After mastering ControlNet, I could reliably generate images that followed specific layouts, maintained consistent character appearances, and respected spatial relationships—capabilities that are essential for professional design work.

Thoughts on the Future of Generative AI

After deep diving into the rabbit hole of Generative AI, the possibilities seem endless. Anyone can create beautiful images, songs, and art with these powerful tools.

×

AI in UX/UI will soon become the norm when UI screen designs can be generated and UX flows can be created with just a click of a button from a prompt. This raises an important question for our field: How will we set ourselves apart as UX/UI designers in this new landscape?

I believe the answer lies in understanding both the technology and human needs at a deeper level. While AI can generate options, designers who understand the technical terminology and concepts behind these tools will be able to direct and refine AI outputs with precision. Our value will shift from pixel-perfect execution to strategic guidance and human-centered evaluation of AI-generated solutions.

Technical Fluency

Understanding AI terminology and concepts will become a core skill for designers to effectively direct these tools

Curation & Refinement

The ability to critically evaluate and refine AI outputs will become more valuable than creation from scratch

Strategic Integration

Knowing when and how to integrate AI tools into the design process will differentiate expert designers

Human Connection

Understanding the nuanced human needs that AI might miss will remain a distinctly human advantage
